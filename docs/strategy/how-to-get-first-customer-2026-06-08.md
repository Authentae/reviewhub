# How to actually get the first paying customer (research, 2026-06-08)

Written while Earth slept, after he sent the first 5 get-reviews emails and
asked: *"can I really earn a dollar off this? research what to improve toward
a successful business model."* This is a no-bias synthesis of outside research
against our own data.

## The one finding that matters most: the channel is wrong, not the product

Across solo-SaaS first-customer research, **cold email is consistently NOT how
first customers are won.** What works, repeatedly:

- **Direct one-to-one conversations** with people who have the problem.
- **Existing network** — first customers usually come from people the founder
  already knows.
- **Free pilots** — set it up for someone, even free, then the first payment
  (even $50/mo) is the real validation.
- **Community engagement** (FB groups, Reddit) and **partnerships**.

Our own data agrees loudly: **5 cold-email waves → 0 replies.** That is not bad
luck or bad copy. It is the channel. A 6th wave (the dental get-reviews test)
is worth watching, but we should **expect** it to underperform and have the
real plan ready.

## What this means for ReviewHub specifically

Earth has two assets the research says to use, that cold email ignores:

1. **He is physically in Bangkok.** He can walk into a dental clinic / café /
   spa, show the product on his phone, and offer to set it up. In-person beats
   cold email by a wide margin for local SMBs.
2. **Thai SMBs live on LINE, not email.** Our own memory says this. Cold
   *email* to Thai owners is doubly wrong — wrong channel AND wrong medium.

## The concrete play (replaces "send more cold emails")

**Stop selling software. Do it for them, free, and show reviews appear.**

1. Pick **1–2 businesses Earth can reach in person or via LINE** (a clinic he
   walks into, or anyone in his network with a Google Business Profile).
2. Offer: *"I'll set this up for you free for 2 weeks. You'll get more Google
   reviews. If you like it, it's $14/mo. If not, no cost."*
3. Set it up FOR them (white-glove). Make the first new review appear fast.
4. That first success → screenshot/testimonial → the case study that makes the
   next ask credible. **This is the asset 6 months of website never produced.**

This is the "personal conversation + free pilot" pattern, adapted to Bangkok.

## Pricing & competition (the model is viable, barely)

SMB review tools range **$9.99–$49/mo** (FiveStarFlow $29, Famewall $9.99,
enterprise like Broadly/Birdeye $249–299). **ReviewHub at $14 is competitively
cheap** — price is not the problem.

But the math is sobering: **$14/mo means ~70 paying customers for $1k MRR.**
Cold email at 0% conversion cannot get there. Personal/pilot/partnership motion
can plausibly get the first 5–10; referrals + case studies compound after that.
Consider whether a higher-touch tier (done-for-you setup at $30–49) fits the
white-glove play better than racing to 70 customers at $14.

Competitor features we should know about: **smart routing** (happy → Google,
unhappy → private), SMS, QR codes. We have review-requests + QR + SMS. (Note:
review-*gating*/routing can violate Google policy — our not-gating may be a
correct honesty choice, not a gap.)

## Retention: when we DO land one, the first 90 days decide everything

**43% of SMB customer losses happen in the first 90 days**, and early churn is
"almost always an onboarding / time-to-value problem." Translation for us:

> The single biggest retention lever is **getting the customer their first new
> Google review FAST** — days, not weeks. Onboarding should be built around that
> one visible win. (The first-customer-onboarding-sequence doc already exists;
> when a customer lands, optimise it for time-to-first-review above all.)

## Honest recommendation (for Earth to decide when awake)

1. **Watch the 5 dental replies (~June 13–15)** — but don't bet on them.
2. **Run the white-glove pilot play** — 1–2 in-person / LINE businesses, set up
   free, prove reviews appear, convert to paid. This is the highest-probability
   path to the actual first dollar.
3. **Verify the money path takes real cash** — one real $14 purchase + refund.
4. **Freeze the website.** It is good enough. More pages ≠ more customers. The
   bottleneck is a real conversation with a real owner, not the site.

## What I did NOT do (deliberately)

I did not ship more marketing pages or polish. My own scrutiny said the site is
over-built for 0 customers; adding more would be motion, not progress. The two
real code gaps on the money path (homepage body, audit demo) are now fixed and
live. Beyond that, the next move is **yours and in-person**, not mine and in-code.
